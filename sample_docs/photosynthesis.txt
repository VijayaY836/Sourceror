Photosynthesis: A Short Primer

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria
convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. It takes place
mainly in the leaves of plants, inside organelles called chloroplasts. The green
pigment chlorophyll, found in the chloroplasts, absorbs light — most strongly in
the blue and red parts of the spectrum — and reflects green light, which is why
most plants appear green.

The overall reaction can be summarised as: six molecules of carbon dioxide plus
six molecules of water, in the presence of light energy, produce one molecule of
glucose and six molecules of oxygen.

Photosynthesis occurs in two stages. The first stage, the light-dependent
reactions, takes place in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast. Here, light
energy splits water molecules (photolysis), releasing oxygen as a by-product and
generating the energy carriers ATP and NADPH.

The second stage, the light-independent reactions (also called the Calvin cycle),
takes place in the stroma of the chloroplast. It does not require light directly.
Instead, it uses the ATP and NADPH produced in the first stage to fix carbon
dioxide into glucose through a series of enzyme-controlled steps.

Several factors affect the rate of photosynthesis. Light intensity, carbon
dioxide concentration, and temperature are the three main limiting factors. As
light intensity increases, the rate of photosynthesis rises until another factor
becomes limiting. Temperature affects the enzymes involved: too cold and they
work slowly, too hot and they begin to denature, typically above about 40 degrees
Celsius for most plants.

Photosynthesis is fundamental to life on Earth. It is the primary source of
oxygen in the atmosphere and forms the base of nearly every food chain, capturing
the solar energy that ultimately feeds most living organisms.
